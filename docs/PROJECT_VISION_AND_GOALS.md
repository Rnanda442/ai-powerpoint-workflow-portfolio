# AI Think Tank Vision And Goals

Last updated: June 8, 2026

Source: Gmail message `Ai portfolio questions and answers to constrain the project website`.
The message contains 63 original questions, 55 follow-up questions, and the
project owner's answers.

## Identity

This is not primarily a finished-project portfolio, recruiting site, or product
catalog. It is a visual AI think tank built from personal experiments.

The projects are evidence and conversation anchors. The main subject is how
prompting, screenshots, files, domain knowledge, Codex, GitHub, scientific
software, visualization, and modern ML workflows can move technical ideas
further.

## Audience

The main audience is:

- DOE and energy managers working near the front of AI integration.
- AI and technical professionals interested in energy and scientific workflows.
- Experienced people who can challenge the ideas with knowledge of ML,
  knowledge graphs, infrastructure, agents, cloud systems, and older tools.
- Curious builders who may adapt the workflows to their own projects.

The site is not optimized for recruiters. The desired outcome is discussion,
learning, collaboration, and useful disagreement.

## Visitor Outcomes

After 30 seconds, a visitor should see several visual AI topics and be able to
say, "I want to discuss that."

After five minutes, a visitor should understand:

- Codex can work with screenshots, files, code, GitHub, and existing artifacts.
- The user's idea and domain knowledge determine what AI is asked to build.
- AI can organize and accelerate work, but it needs technical guidance.
- Current use and future ML possibilities are different.
- Every future idea should connect to a real bottleneck, product, public value,
  energy need, or economic opportunity.

## Core Claim

AI is a powerful tool, but useful products depend on the user's ideas, domain
knowledge, workflow design, validation, and understanding of available ML
approaches.

## Tone

- Curious, informal, visual, and technically grounded.
- Fact-based where evidence exists.
- Opinionated enough to start discussion.
- Honest about prompting being the main workflow.
- Open about unfinished work and uncertainty.
- Never written as if the owner is already an advanced ML authority.

## Content Hierarchy

Every topic should follow this order:

1. A question worth discussing.
2. A visual system or motion concept.
3. A real project example.
4. What tools and AI actions were used.
5. What the human supplied or validated.
6. A specific industry or scientific bottleneck.
7. A technically plausible future ML workflow.
8. A visible output, product, benefit, or decision.
9. A prompt inviting the audience to challenge or extend the idea.

## Visual Rules

- Prefer images, crops, arrows, dots, branches, icons, maps, and motion.
- Use one-word or short-phrase labels directly on the visual.
- Do not use paragraphs to explain a workflow that can be drawn.
- Crop screenshots to the evidence that matters.
- Connect abstract diagrams to real project imagery.
- Show current AI use and future ML as two visibly different states.
- Use gray for uncertainty and unresolved questions.
- Use gates for human review, leakage checks, rubrics, and validation.
- Use branching paths when a workflow has alternative outputs or decisions.
- Technical depth belongs after the visual, not before it.

## Topic Priorities

The site should open as a wall of discussion topics rather than a ranked project
portfolio. Strong examples include:

- AI for public energy data and 3D subsurface screening.
- How scientific software agents learn from human demonstrations.
- Knowledge graphs and visual reasoning for critical minerals.
- AI-assisted app building, cloud workflows, and honest model evaluation.
- Instrument properties to rock-property and resource maps.
- Seismic automation with visible uncertainty.
- Spatial ML transfer, leakage, and residual mapping.

No single project is the centerpiece. Visitors choose the topic relevant to
their interests.

## Evidence And Credibility

Credibility should come from:

- Real maps, graphs, screenshots, outputs, tables, and interactions.
- Clear explanation of what AI actually did.
- Clear explanation of what the user knew, decided, or checked.
- Source-backed datasets and repeated review where scientifically relevant.
- Honest labeling of current work versus speculative future ML.
- Visible failure risks such as leakage, weak labels, uncertain interpretation,
  and incomplete validation.

Local filesystem paths and inventory tables are build details. They should not
dominate the public experience.

## Conversion

The desired final action is:

**Share an idea, correction, technical approach, or relevant experience.**

Contact language should invite conversation, not employment.

## Hard Constraints

- Topics before projects.
- Visuals before prose.
- Specific AI/ML ideas before generic "AI can help" language.
- Discussion before self-promotion.
- Real evidence before decoration.
- Honest uncertainty before polished claims.
- No recruiter-specific path.
- No implication that every project is complete or deployed.
